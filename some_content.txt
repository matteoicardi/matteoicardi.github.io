Comprehensive Academic Profile: Professor Matteo Icardi

1. Professional Identity and Digital Strategic Positioning

Professor Matteo Icardi is a pioneer in the digitalization of the energy transition, operating at the critical intersection of mathematical engineering, computational fluid dynamics (CFD), and multiscale modeling. As an Associate Professor at the University of Nottingham and a core member of the GeoEnergy Research Centre (GERC), he directs a research portfolio dedicated to resolving the complex interplay between microscopic physics and macroscopic system performance. His work is instrumental in overcoming the inherent computational bottlenecks of classical engineering simulations through the strategic deployment of uncertainty quantification (UQ), model reduction, and Scientific Machine Learning (SciML).

2. Biographical Narrative and Academic Foundations

The strategic value of an international academic trajectory lies in the cross-pollination of methodological traditions. Professor Icardi’s career is a testament to this, blending Italian mathematical rigor with American stochastic numerics and the advanced HPC capabilities of the Middle East.

* The Foundational Era (Politecnico di Torino): Rooted in the Italian tradition of mathematical engineering, Icardi’s early career focused on multiphase systems. His doctoral research provided critical advancements in tracking particle size distributions in turbulent flows, integrating Large Eddy Simulation (LES) with the Quadrature Method of Moments (QMOM).
* Global Specialization (UT Austin and KAUST): His tenure at the Institute for Computational Engineering and Sciences (ICES) and the STOCHNUM group at KAUST allowed him to synthesize complex fluid dynamics with advanced Uncertainty Quantification. This phase was pivotal in mastering Multilevel Monte Carlo (MLMC) methods, enabling the calibration of models against sparse, noisy experimental data.
* UK Leadership (Warwick and Nottingham): After serving as the Zeeman Lecturer at Warwick, he joined the University of Nottingham in 2017. He has since established the MultiForm group as a hub for multiscale research, ascending to Associate Professor in 2022 and assuming steering roles within the GeoEnergy Research Centre.

3. Core Research Pillars and Methodological Frameworks

In the context of the global energy transition and the emergence of Digital Twins, multiscale simulation is the primary vehicle for scientific discovery. Professor Icardi’s research operationalizes this through four strategic "Deep Dives":

1. Multiscale Modeling & Coarse-Graining: This pillar focuses on deriving macroscopic equations from microscopic phenomena. By analyzing "memory terms" in coarse-grained models (e.g., the Markovian approximation), Professor Icardi enables field-scale predictions that would otherwise require centuries of computation to resolve at the pore scale.
2. Porous Media & Subsurface Dynamics: His work applies upscaling methods to fractured reservoirs and unconventional risks, directly impacting Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) and Contaminant Hydrology. This pillar was the cornerstone of the EU H2020 SECURe project, addressing how pollutants migrate through heterogeneous geological formations.
3. Uncertainty Quantification (UQ) & Stochastic Numerics: Professor Icardi utilizes Multilevel Monte Carlo (MLMC) to optimize computational budgets. By combining massive cohorts of low-resolution simulations with targeted high-resolution samples, he quantifies uncertainty in parameters like permeability: E[Q_L] = E[Q_0] + \sum_{l=1}^L E[Q_l - Q_{l-1}]
4. Scientific Machine Learning (SciML): Representing a cutting-edge shift in the field, his recent work—highlighted in his 2025 publication in Computer Methods in Applied Mechanics and Engineering—uses Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) to solve inverse transport problems. By embedding PDEs into the loss function, he reconstructs flow fields from sparse data: Loss = Loss_{data} + \lambda Loss_{PDE}

Methodological Versatility: Beyond energy, Icardi’s mathematical polymathy is evident in his application of Biot’s consolidation theory to brain tissue deformation for neurosurgery and the modeling of thermal ablation for tumor treatment, where he accounts for vapor-mediated heat transport.

4. The MultiForm Research Group: Structure and Leadership

The MultiForm (Multiscale Fluid Dynamics and Porous Media) group serves as an interdisciplinary engine within the School of Mathematical Sciences. It connects rigorous theory to Faculty of Engineering applications and coordinates the "Heterogeneous Media Research Theme" at Nottingham.

Research Supervision and Key Projects

* Robert Barnett (PhD, 2019–2023): Developed open-source simulation frameworks for pore-scale solid-liquid systems and electrokinetic flows.
* Eugenio Pescimoro (PhD, 2019–2022): Focused on non-Fickian transport and emergence of anomalous dispersion in geological media.
* Salihu Yakubu (PhD, 2022–2025): Investigating thin film and vertically integrated flow models.
* Tobias Long (PhD, 2021–2023): Advancing the Cumulative Radon Transform for CFD model reduction.
* Abhilash Sreekumar (PhD, 2018–2021): Researching vibro-acoustic properties of porous materials.
